Lola’s Main Archetype


Lola’s main archetype is, perhaps, a rebel. Like all other people participating in drug dealing, she is against the government and the law, which forbids drug dealing especially for meth and heroin. This means that drug dealers and people participating in such acts would be relatively isolated from the “normal” society, which views drugs as greatly harmful as they disrupt the order of society. That means the people with connections to drugs would have to take risks and would likely be brave, individual, and illegal. This makes Lola especially fit for the rebel archetype. Even more “rebel” than other low-ranked drug participators, such as the “muscle man” and her soldiers, she also has more individuality as she does not want to completely follow Eldridge or the cartel and become a mere pawn of theirs. She wants to create her kingdom and that makes her even more like a “rebel”.

Lola’s Nondominant Archetype

Lola’s Nondominant Archetype is the archetype of caregiver. This archetype would completely contradict her major archetype, which is the rebel/outlaw. Unlike the rebels, the caregiver would be selfless and loyal and would lack personal ambitions. This caregiver trait of Lola is nondominant, that most of the time Lola is a rebel but for various occasions, Lola would be caregiving. Such occasions can be seen in Lola’s relationship with Lucy Amaros. For Lucy, Lola was caregiving on nearly all occasions, including sometimes taking over her rebel trait and role to save Lucy and trying to protect her all the time, making Lucy on a high priority of her actions. However, this caregiving trait may also be seen in her relationship with Hector, her baby brother. She would try to raise her brother like a mother and protect his brother, even killing people when she finds Hector being threatened. However, at other times, when Hector did not obey her rebel leader’s role, she would punish Hector with no hesitation.

Conflicts that move the plot

The first conflict that moved the plot in the relationship with Lola was her conflict with the “smaller society”, which is the leaders of drug dealing in Los Angeles, including Eldridge Waterson and Juan Gomez. This conflict was majorly due to her rebel trait and her ambitions, which motivated her to get more power and control over drug exchange. Thus, she would try to live on and try to defeat Eldridge and Juan to become the ultimate dealer of Los Angeles. This would move Lola to try to live on, trying to live between Juan and Eldridge, and lastly burn Eldridge’s warehouse and kill Juan.

The second conflict that moved the plot was Lola’s conflict with Hector. Hector was Lola’s baby brother, and she raised Hector just like her child. She gives care to Hector and tries to protect Hector when she feels Carlos is harming Hector. However, when Hector disobeys Lola’s instructions, first when he gives mercy in the drug deal, and second when he jumps up to fight Darrel King, Lola does not try to protect Hector anymore. For the former conflict, Lola punished Hector by cutting off his trigger finger, and for the second occasion, Lola punished Hector by sending Hector to jail. This would move the plot as her conflict with Hector forced Lola into some kind of desperation. She was assigned by the cartel to intercept the drug deal between Darrel King and Mr. X, but because of Hector, she fails, when she tries to make a deal with Darrel in the later part of the story, because of Hector, she has to kill Darrel King, which pushes her to a spot that both Eldridge and Juan did not like her.

The third conflict that moved the plot was Lola’s conflict with herself. Her role in the society of Los Angeles was an outlaw who tried to sell drugs, while in the drug society, she was just a pawn who should obey anything her leader assigned. However, instead of being lawless and ambitious, on some occasions, she was caregiving, especially when she was interacting with Lucy Amaros. On these occasions, she tried to not expose Lucy to the dark drug world and protected Lucy, sometimes even harming herself.

Tension or Correspondence

Dichotomy 1: She is the leader of a gang that sells drugs, but she would not trust or feel pity for any drug addicts, thus hating her customers. Thus, this shows the complexity of Lola, she has relatively high moral standards, but her role in society is to supply the people she dislikes with drugs.
